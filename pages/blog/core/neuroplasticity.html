Neuroplasticity and Growth Mindset

Neuroplasticity

Discuss how understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefits people

The main principle of neuroplasticity is that intelligence isn't fixed, you can change it by practising - by not giving up. This can benefit those that think they're bad at something, because chances are they're not. They just haven't spent as much time on it as others and they give up because they aren't performing well right now. Believing intelligence is fixed is a self-fulfilling prophecy. You aren't good at something, you don't believe you ever will be, you stop trying, and you never get better. Believeing in malleable intelligence is also a self-fulfilling prophecy. You aren't good at something, you believe you can get better, you keep trying, and you do get better.

Growth Mindset

Discuss what it is and why it is relevant

A growth mindset is a mindset that believes that intelligence, creativity, or any other metric of ability, can be improved with time and practice. It is the antithesis of a fixed mindset which believes that intelligence, etc. is fixed, unchanging. This mindset can lead to people feeling the need to compare themselves to others, to avoid making mistakes, to avoid challenges (because that would increase the chance of mistakes). A growth mindset on the other hand promotes seeking out challenges as a medium for growth. It is likely that for some things you already have a growth mindset, but for others you could have a fixed mindset, for example, I tend to try to avoid writing blogs like this because I think I'm bad at writing blogs - but if I ran into a difficult maths or coding problem I would look for ways to solve it because I think I'm good at it. It is super relevant because being aware that I don't have a growth mindset in some areas and being able to try applying when I feel stuck is going to help immensely with the feeling of wanting to give up.

My understanding before doing this research wasn't quite a growth mindset but it was leaning towards it.

How will you integrate growth mindset into your learning journey?

Instead I am going to balance work I can do easily and work I could get stuck on, not giving either one too much time. On top of that I should try to view blog writing and other core exercises as a challenge rather than a chore.
